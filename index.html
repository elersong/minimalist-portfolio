The Value of Changing the Medium

23 Feb 2021

As much as I hate to admit it, my dad was right. Back in grade school, he would drone on and on about the value of drilling concepts until they became second-nature, turning them over in my mind until I extend past a simple knowledge and into true understanding. At the time, I knew everything about everything. Thankfully, I've grown some humility since then.

I probably would have been a much better student as a teenager had I heeded his advice. When he would take time to interview me about my school lessons, he would inevitably ask me how I had studied. Once I had given him my faithfully mediocre response, he would ask me for my notes and begin to quiz me. He'd ask questions beyond the text, the kind that required me to be familiar not only with the words on the page, but the relationships between their underlying ideas.

While I found it irritating at the time, often whining about how my teachers didn't require me to know the material in that way, I can retrospectively see that he was preparing me for the world I would enter. As an adult, I often find myself probing deeper into ideas to build a palpable mastery. When I do, I can very often hear my dad's voice affirming, "See? Those are the things you can do when you're comfortable with the idea, because you changed up the medium."

As a kid, "changing the medium" meant rewriting my notes over and over, using flashcards or reading into a voice recorder to listen to the main points of the lesson while I did other things. This forced my mind to acclimate to differing ways of interacting with the same data, and as a result, I learned to build mastery much more quickly than my peers, who paid attention to only the required manner of learning.

Now, as an adult (legally speaking, anyway) in a world as complicated as it is unforgiving, I've grown to greatly appreciate the time my dad spent teaching me that lesson. My universe is a little easier to traverse than it otherwise could have been. Thanks, dad.

Why Coding is Like Cooking

25 Feb 2021

Anybody who has weilded a semicolon has heard it before... "Oh I don't understand how you could enjoy that. Programming is so complicated. I could never." And plenty of us have had the thought in response, "Well, if you should ever change your mind, don't call me." But despite the perceived difficulty of taming the wild magic internet box, those who often interface with it are in-tune with the simplicity of its pieces. Each one serves a specific purpose, which can be reduced to a generally simple task, much like another, more commonly-mastered, skill.

There's a certain nefarious gratification I gain from impressing friends and family using skills taught in CS-101, but if I'm peculiarly honest about it, I'm certain that the same skills that make a great cook also result in a great programmer. If that were to become common knowledge, I'd end up with fewer cookies and slices of cake, which is simply unacceptable.

Regardless, I firmly believe that while the knowledge of knife skills and familiarity with complementary flavor proviles may not get you a promotion in the tech field, both activities build upon common foundations. Hear me out. Both disciplines require 10k hours of improvement and build complexity from the elegant balance of small, simple steps. If I were to describe a paste made of salt, water and flour, wrapped around butter and smooshed flat, you may not immediately think of a croissant or puff pastry, but that's all it is. Likewise, if I mentioned a network of brief, self-organizing messages, zipping around the world, managing billions of concurrent connections and influencing everything from politics to world trade, you may not think of Twitter, yet here we are.

In both cases, the end result can easily be thought of as far greater than the sum of numerous small, simple parts. But although the final products may appear to exist out of the realm of possibility for the average bear, none of the individual steps to close the gap between the starting point and the destination are particularly difficult to understand. The most basic skill underpinning both disciplines is simply the ability to divide a large issue into a series of smaller and smaller issues, becoming easier and easier to resolve with each iteration.

So, I wasn't kidding. If it makes sense to you to start with a general plan, refining it to get a little closer to your target with each step you take, I would say that you're already largely qualified to attempt computer programming. But then again, what do I know?

Frustration is the Mother of Creativity

29 Feb 2021

On days when I've spent hours staring blankly at an angry console, praying to whomever will listen that 305th attempt would somehow be different than the 304th, I tend to have my best ideas. You may not be willing to stake your retirement account in angel investment on that version of me, and I wouldn't either if I hadn't lived through it so many times. The fact, however, still stands that the bottom of the barrel is where the best ideas are born.

It's really not the months of Array-function drills or masterclasses I've given my dogs on the principles of asynchronous techniques that have brought me the most value as a developer. Like most people (or so I would assume), I can't easily notice everything, and bugs seem to naturally camouflage themselves within the shadows of my mind's eye. It's only once I've checked all the usual hiding spots that I can begin to see my projects as they truly are built, as opposed to the assumptions I can usually get away with making about them.

Those moments, in my opinion, are when I truly grow as an engineer. That's when I can find out how sharp my skills really are. I'm all out of tricks and cheap workarounds, and I have to form a concept that's entirely new to me. Unfortunately for all my patience, abstracting a new idea out of the ether is a painfully slow process, fraught with head-banging, tears and gnashing of teeth. The pride that emerges from a defeated challenge, however, makes every eternal moment entirely worth it... almost...
